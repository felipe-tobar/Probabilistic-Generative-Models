\chapter{Flow-based models}

\textbf{NB:} This is based on \cite{papamakarios2021normalizing}, \cite{lipman2023flowmatching}, \cite{lipman2024flowmatchingtutorial}, \cite{grathwohl2019ffjord},  \cite{tong2023improving},  \cite{chen2018neuralode}, and \cite{liu2022rectified}.


\paragraph{Motivation.}

We aim to define a distribution over $\x\in\R^D$. Rather than parametrising it directly, we will express $\x$ as a transformation of a random vector $\u$. That is,
\[\x = T(\u), \qquad \u \sim p_\u(\u).\] If the map and the base (or source) measure have parameters, say, $\phi$ and $\psi$ respectively, they will be the parameters of the target measure denoted $p(\x)$.

Formally, the induced model will be the pushforward measure of $p_\u$ through $T$, that is,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:push_forward_density}
    \int_A p(\x)\, d\x
=
\int_{T^{-1}(A)} p_\u(\u)\, d\u ,
\qquad
\forall A \subset \mathbb{R}^D \text{ measurable}.    
\end{equation}


This is somehow an extension of the setting we adopted in the variational autoencoder formulation, where the base distribution was assumed to be Gaussian, the transformation $T(\cdot)$ a neural network, and the learning paradigm was variational inference. The extensions to be considered in this chapter will take two directions. First, by imposing continuity and invertibility conditions of the map, the resulting model can be trained via maximum likelihood, provided the inverse of the map can be computed. Second, we will consider an approach where the map is broken down in stages, initially considering a compositional construction of the map and then a continuous version; we will see that this perspective calls for an entirely new learning strategy. 


\section{Normalising flows}

We will now focus on a case characterised by a clear (and arguably restrictive) condition on $T$: we will require it to be a diffeomorphism. A definition is given as follows as a reminder.

\begin{definition}[Diffeomorphism]
Let $T:\mathbb{R}^D \to \mathbb{R}^D$ be a map. We say that $T$ is a \emph{diffeomorphism} if it is bijective, continuously differentiable, and its inverse $T^{-1}$ is also continuously differentiable. Equivalently, $T$ is a $\cC^1$ bijection with a $\cC^1$ inverse. In particular, the Jacobian matrix $\nabla T(\u)$ exists everywhere and is non-singular, i.e.\ $\det \nabla T(\u) \neq 0$ for all $\u \in \mathbb{R}^D$.
\end{definition}

The comment about $T$ being a bijective map with a well defined Jacobian is crucial in this setting, as that enables us to express the induced density over $\x = T(\u)$ via the change-of-variables theorem in terms of the base measure $p_\u$ as follows 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:CoV_invT}    
p(\x)
=
p_\u\!\left(T^{-1}(\x)\right)
\left|
\det \nabla T^{-1}(\x)
\right|,
\end{equation}
or equivalently, as a function of the source variable $\u$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:CoV_T}    
p(\x)
=
p_\u(\u)
\left|
\det \nabla T(\u)
\right|^{-1}.
\end{equation}


Some relevant remarks mentioned as follows. 

\begin{remark}[Mass-preserving warping]
The map $T$ can be interpreted as a warping of the ambient space $\cX$, which reorganises the probability mass of $p_\u$ into the target density $p$. Since probability mass must be preserved, the transformation only \emph{transports} mass; it neither creates nor destroys it. Therefore, local changes in volume induced by the map must be reflected in the resulting density, and this is precisely the role of the Jacobian factor $\det \nabla T^{-1}(\x)$ in eq.~\eqref{eq:CoV_invT} above. If the transformation locally compesses the space, the density must increase accordingly to preserve mass; conversely, if the space is locally expanded, the density must decrease.
\end{remark}


\begin{remark}[Training: Closed form likelihood]
The identity in eq.~\eqref{eq:CoV_invT} allows for the likelihood, given observations $\x$, to be calculated in closed form, provided that both the inverse map $T^{-1}$ and the determinant of its Jacobian can be evaluated.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Sampling]
Under the proposed methodology, generating samples from $\x\sim p(\x)$ is direct by first sampling $\u\sim p_\u$, and then evaluating $\x = T(\u)$. This can be done efficiently if the source measure is a simple density (e.g., Gaussian or uniform), and if the map $T$ can be evaluated.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Normalising flow]
Note that either the direct map $T:\u\to\x$ (when the aim is sampling) or the reverse one $T^{-1}:\x\to\u$ (when the aim is learning) need to be available. Since the case is usually the latter, the inverse map is usually parametrised via $\phi=T^{-1}$, and the base measure is considered to be Gaussian. Therefore, the role of $\phi$ is to convert an arbitrary data distribution $p(\x)$ into a Normal variable, hence the name for $\phi$ as normalising flow.
\end{remark}




\paragraph{Construction of expressive flows.}

At this stage, it is natural to ask how expressive the normalising flow construction is. In other words, given an arbitrary pair of densities $p_\u(\u), p(\x)$, does a map $T$ pushing the former to the latter always exist? For the scalar case, i.e., $\x\in\R$, verifying this is straightforward: when the densities are smooth, we can consider the mononote rearrangement given by 
\begin{equation}
    T(\u) = F^{-1}_\x(F_\u(\u)),
\end{equation}
where $F_\x$ and $F_\u$ are the cumulative distribution functions of $\x$ and $\u$ respectively. Intuitively, this flow is pushing the mass from $p_\u$ to a uniform distribution using $F_\u(\cdot)$, and then pushing it from there to $p$ via $F^{-1}_\x(\cdot)$. 


Regarding the non-scalar case, if the base measure \(p_\u\) is absolutely continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure, then there exists a map satisfying eq.~\eqref{eq:push_forward_density}. One important existence result is provided by Brenier's theorem (see the OT chapter). In particular, when both measures are absolutely continuous and the transport cost is quadratic, the optimal transport map exists, is unique under suitable conditions, and is given by the gradient of a convex potential. This provides a particularly relevant class of transport maps in the context of normalising flows.

In addition to Brenier's theoretical guarantees, further insight into the expressivity of normalising flows can be gained by trying to adapt the monotone arrangement concept to the vector-valued case. Let us first observe that the monotone arrangement cannot be applied to vectors because, although the multivariate CDF is well defined and given by 
\begin{equation}
    F_\x(\x) = \int_{(-\infty,x_1)\times\cdots\times (-\infty,x_D)}p(\x)\d\x,
\end{equation}
it is not invertible and, thus, a monotone arrangement cannot be constructed (there is, in particular, no concept of motononicity in $\R^D$).

An alternative is to consider the triangular flow given by the \emph{Knothe--Rosenblatt rearrangement}---see \cite[Ch.~2]{villani2009optimal}. In this construction the
transport map is triangular, meaning that each coordinate depends
only on the current and previous ones,
\[
T(\u)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
T_1(u_1)\\
T_2(u_1,u_2)\\
\vdots\\
T_D(u_1,\ldots,u_D)
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The map is constructed recursively using conditional CDFs. Writing
the base density as
\[
p_\u(\u)
=
p_{u,1}(u_1)
\prod_{k=2}^D
p_{u,k}(u_k \mid u_{1:k-1}),
\]
define the conditional CDFs
\[
F_{u,k}(t \mid u_{1:k-1})
=
\int_{-\infty}^{t}
p_{u,k}(s \mid u_{1:k-1})\,ds,
\]
and similarly for the target density $p(\x)$,
\[
F_k(t \mid x_{1:k-1})
=
\int_{-\infty}^{t}
p_k(s \mid x_{1:k-1})\,ds .
\]

The transport map is then defined recursively as
\[
x_k
=
F_k^{-1}\!\left(
F_{u,k}(u_k \mid u_{1:k-1})
\mid x_{1:k-1}
\right),
\qquad k=1,\ldots,D .
\]

Intuitively, each step first maps $u_k$ to a uniform variable via the
conditional CDF of the base distribution and then maps it to the
correct conditional distribution of the target via the inverse
conditional CDF. This construction yields a diffeomorphic triangular
map $T$ satisfying $T_\# p_\u = p$, showing that flows are expressive
enough to represent arbitrary smooth densities.

This triangular structure is particularly attractive from the
perspective of the change-of-variables formula. Indeed, the Jacobian
matrix $\nabla T(\u)$ is triangular, and therefore its determinant is
simply the product of its diagonal entries,
\[
\det \nabla T(\u)
=
\prod_{k=1}^D
\frac{\partial T_k}{\partial u_k}(u_{1:k}).
\]
As a result, the Jacobian determinant required in
eq.~\eqref{eq:CoV_T} can be computed efficiently, which facilitates training. Additionally, since the inverse of a triangular map is also  triangular, the same computational advantage holds when evaluating the likelihood
via the inverse transformation in eq.~\eqref{eq:CoV_invT}.


Another natural way to construct expressive flows is to build the map as a
composition of simpler diffeomorphisms. Concretely, write
\[
T = T_K \circ \cdots \circ T_1,
\]
where each $T_k : \R^D \to \R^D$ is an invertible transformation with a
tractable Jacobian determinant. If we define the intermediate variables
$\z_0=\u$, $\z_k = T_k(\z_{k-1})$ for $k=1,\ldots,K$, and $\z_K=\x$, then the
change-of-variables formula can be applied recursively, yielding
\[
\log p(\x)
=
\log p_\u(\u)
-
\sum_{k=1}^K
\log \left|\det \nabla T_k(\z_{k-1})\right|.
\]
This compositional construction allows complex transformations to be
obtained by stacking simple building blocks, while keeping likelihood
evaluation tractable.

\section{Continuous-time flows}

We now consider a continuous version of the flow, where rather than a collection of sequential transformations as in the compositional construction, we will implement flows that model the (infinitesimal) evolution of the variables. To this end, consider a time-indexed random variable $\z_t$, with
$t\in[0,1]$, obtained by evolving the initial random variable
$\u \sim p_\u$ through a deterministic flow. In particular,
\[
\z_0 = \u,
\qquad
\z_1 = \x .
\]

Unlike discrete flows, where the transformation is applied from one variable to the next, we now model the temporal evolution of $\z_t$, that is, 
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:ODE}
    \frac{\d\z_t}{\dt} = g_\phi(t,\z_t).   
\end{equation}
 Here, we will require that the function $g_\phi: [0,1] \times \R^D \to \R^D$ is uniformly Lipschitz continuous in $\z$ and continuous in $t$, so that the ODE admits a unique solution. Furthermore, we will refer to the function $g_\phi(\cdot,\cdot)$ as \emph{velocity field}.

\begin{remark}[Neural ordinary differential equations]
The dynamics above can be interpreted as a Neural ODE. To see this, recall that a deep residual network updates a hidden state through layers of the form
\[
\z_{k+1} = \z_k + h\, g_\phi(t_k, \z_k),
\]
where $h$ is a small step size and $g_\phi$ is typically implemented as a neural network. If we interpret the layer index as a discretised time variable $t_k = kh$, this recursion corresponds to a forward
Euler discretisation of the differential equation
\[
\frac{\d \z_t}{\d t} = g_\phi(t,\z_t).
\]
In the limit, as the number of layers increases and the step size
$h\to0$, the residual network converges to the continuous-time
dynamics above. Neural ODEs \cite{chen2018neuralode} therefore provide a continuous-depth analogue of deep networks, where the transformation between input and output is defined implicitly as the solution of a learned dynamical
system.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Evolution of densities.}
The ODE in  eq.~\eqref{eq:ODE} describes how individual samples evolve. A natural question, however, is how the corresponding probability density evolves over time based on those dynamics. Let $p_t(\z)$ denote the density of $\z_t$. Since the flow
simply transports probability mass without creating or destroying it,
the density must satisfy the \emph{continuity equation} \cite[Ch.~5]{villani2009optimal}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:continuity}
\partial_t p_t(\z)
+
\nabla_\z \cdot \big(p_t(\z)\, g_\phi(t,\z)\big)
=
0.
\end{equation}
This equation expresses conservation of probability mass: the change in
density at a point is determined by the divergence of the probability
flux $p_t(\z) g_\phi(t,\z)$.

While eq.~\eqref{eq:continuity} describes the evolution of the full density field, understanding the change of the density along individual trajectories of the flow is insightful. To this end, we will let $\z_t$ follow the dynamics above and inspect the time derivative of the log-density along this trajectory.

Expanding the divergence in eq.~\eqref{eq:continuity} using the product
rule yields
\[
\partial_t p_t(\z)
+
g_\phi(t,\z)\cdot\nabla_\z p_t(\z)
+
p_t(\z)\,\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z)
=
0.
\]
Now consider a trajectory $\z_t$ satisfying
\[
\frac{\d \z_t}{\d t} = g_\phi(t,\z_t).
\]
By the chain rule, the total derivative of the density along the
trajectory is
\[
\frac{\d}{\d t} p_t(\z_t)
=
\partial_t p_t(\z_t)
+
g_\phi(t,\z_t)\cdot\nabla_\z p_t(\z_t).
\]
Substituting this expression into the expanded continuity equation
gives
\[
\frac{\d}{\d t} p_t(\z_t)
+
p_t(\z_t)\,\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z_t)
=
0.
\]
Dividing by $p_t(\z_t)$ yields
\[
\frac{\d}{\d t} \log p_t(\z_t)
=
-\,\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z_t).
\]
Finally, recalling that the divergence of a vector field equals the
trace of its Jacobian,
\[
\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z)
=
\mathrm{Tr}\!\left(
\frac{\partial g_\phi}{\partial \z}(t,\z)
\right),
\]
we obtain
\[
\frac{\d}{\d t} \log p_t(\z_t)
=
- \mathrm{Tr}\!\left(
\frac{\partial g_\phi}{\partial \z}(t,\z_t)
\right).
\]

\begin{remark}[Instantaneous change-of-variables formula]
The above identity is known as the \emph{instantaneous change-of-variables} formula. It can be interpreted as the continuous-time analogue of the Jacobian determinant appearing in discrete normalising flows: the divergence of the velocity field $g_\phi$ measures the instantaneous expansion or contraction of volume elements along the flow.
\end{remark}
 


\paragraph{Continuous vs discrete normalising flows.}
The formulation above highlights the appeal of continuous normalising flows. First, rather than specifying a finite composition of transformations $T = T_K \circ \cdots \circ T_1$, the flow is defined through the solution of a differential equation, which can be interpreted as the limit of infinitely many infinitesimal transformations. In practice, the effective number of transformations is determined adaptively by the numerical ODE solver, rather than being fixed a priori by the architecture.

Second, continuous flows avoid the need to explicitly construct inverse maps. Both sampling and density evaluation reduce to solving an initial value problem for the state $\z_t$ and the log-density evolution, removing the structural constraints required in discrete flows to ensure the existence (and tractability) of inverses.

These advantages come with some drawbacks. Solving the ODE typically requires many evaluations of the vector field $g_\phi$, making training considerably more expensive than in discrete flows. Moreover, the computation of the divergence term $\mathrm{Tr}(\partial g_\phi/\partial \z)$ can be costly in high-dimensional settings and often requires stochastic trace estimators. Finally, the accuracy of the model depends on the numerical integration scheme, introducing additional computational overhead and potential numerical error.

\begin{remark}[Free-form Jacobian of Reversible Dynamics (FFJORD)]
A major practical difficulty in continuous normalising flows is the computation of the divergence term $\mathrm{Tr}(\partial g_\phi/\partial \z)$ appearing in the instantaneous change-of-variables formula. In high dimensions, explicitly forming the Jacobian is prohibitively expensive. The FFJORD method \cite{grathwohl2019ffjord} addresses this issue by estimating the trace using Hutchinson's stochastic trace estimator,
\[
\mathrm{Tr}(A) = \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon}[\epsilon^\top A \epsilon],
\]
which allows the divergence to be computed using Jacobian--vector products obtainable through automatic differentiation. This enables the use of unrestricted neural vector fields $g_\phi$ while keeping the computational cost manageable.
\end{remark}

We next review an alternative formulation to specify an appropriate flow $\phi$, by directly learning the velocity field through matching it to a prescribed probability flow between simple and target distributions.

\section{Flow matching}

We introduce an updated notation for ease of presentation in this part:
\begin{itemize}
    \item we refer to the source and target densities as $p(\x)$ and $q(\x)$ respectively,
    \item we no longer refer to a source RV $\u$ or the stochastic process $\z_t$, but only to the variable $\x$ and a family of densities defined over it, as explained next.
\end{itemize}

The evolution from the source to the target will be defined in terms of a probability path $\{p_t\}_{t\in[0,1]}$,  that is, a family of densities fulfilling the boundary conditions
    \[
    p_0(\x) = p(\x), 
    \qquad
    p_1(\x) = q(\x).
    \]
We assume that such a path can be realised through a time-dependent flow
$\phi_t:\R^D \to \R^D$ 
that transports the mass from $p$ (at $t=0$) to $q$ (at $t=1$), or arbitrarily close. Additionally, we also assume that the flow is in turn generated by a  velocity field 
$\v:[0,1]\times\R^D\to\R^D$
according to the ODE
\[
\frac{\d}{\d t}\phi_t(\x)
=
\v_t(\phi_t(\x)),
\qquad
\phi_0(\x)=\x.
\]
For a particle $\x\sim p_0(\x) = p(\x)$, the trajectory $t \mapsto \phi_t(\x)$ describes the evolution of the particle under the velocity field $\v_t$, where the density of $\x$ evolves along the prescribed probability path
$\{p_t\}_{t\in[0,1]}$. 
As before, we say that the velocity field $\v_t$ \emph{generates} the probability path $p_t$ iff the pair $(p_t,\v_t)$ satisfies the continuity equation in eq.~\eqref{eq:continuity}.

\begin{remark}
    Flow matching methods do not parametrise the flow $\phi_t$ directly. Instead, they learn a parametrised vector field $\v_\theta(t,\x)$, which generates the desired probability path. Once the vector field is learned, the corresponding flow $\phi_t$ is obtained by solving the associated ODE.
\end{remark}


\paragraph{Training loss.} To learn the vector field $\v_\theta(x,t)$, \cite{lipman2023flowmatching} proposes the flow-matching objective given by 
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:FM_loss}    
    \cL_\text{FM}(\theta) \defeq \mathbb{E}_{t\sim\uni{0,1},\x\sim p_t(\x)}||\v_\theta(x,t) - \v_t(\x)||^2,
\end{equation}
where $\v_t(\x)$ is a vector field that generates the desired probability path. Notice that this is a supervised learning objective that assumes that we have access to a valid field $\v_t(\x)$, where the parametric field $\v_\theta(x,t)$ is \emph{regressed} onto $\v_t(\x)$. This means that if $ \cL_\text{FM}(\theta) = 0$, then $\v_\theta(x,t)$ also generates the desired probability path. 

\begin{remark}[On the expectation in the flow-matching loss]
The objective in eq.~\eqref{eq:FM_loss} averages the discrepancy between the parametric field $\v_\theta(\x,t)$ and the target field $\v_t(\x)$ over the union of the supports across the probability path. At each point in this set, the loss is weighted by the density $p_t(\x)$, thus placing greater emphasis on regions where probability mass is concentrated.
\end{remark}

Though interpretable and simple, the FM training loss is rather naive: it is intractable in its own since neither $p_t$ nor $\v_t$ are available. In particular, there are several probability paths such that $p_1(\x) = q(\x)$ for a given $p_0$, and also several vector fields that can (perhaps redundantly) move mass according the source and target measures. The following approach takes a conditional perspective to circumvent this problem. 

\paragraph{Conditional probability paths.} Consider a particular data sample $\x_1\sim q(\x)$, and construct the conditional probability  path $p_t(\x \mid \x_1) $ with the boundary conditions\footnote{We start conditioning the path here to a terminal value $\x_1$, however, the conditioning statement can be much more flexible than that. We will revisit more expressive conditioning elements later in this section.}
\begin{align}
    t=0 : p_0(\x \mid \x_1) &= p(\x)\quad \leftarrow \quad \text{the source distribution}\\
    t=1 : p_1(\x \mid \x_1) &= \cN(\x \mid \x_1,\sigma^2\I) \quad \leftarrow \quad \text{with small } \sigma^2.
\end{align}
Conditional to the terminal sample, the evolution of the distribution above describes how samples from the source distribution flow towards a neighbourhood around $\x_1$ at $t=1$, rather than transporting mass directly toward the full data distribution. These trajectories, induced by each datapoint, can be then averaged over $\x_1 \sim p(\x_1)$ to recover a marginal probability path. That is, 
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:marginal_prob_path}
    p_t(\x) = \int p_t(\x \mid \x_1) q(\x_1)\d\x_1.
\end{equation}
The initial and terminal conditions for this marginal probability path are 
\begin{align}
    t=0 : p_0(\x) &=  \int p(\x) q(\x_1)\d\x_1 = p(\x)\\
    t=1 : p_1(\x) &=  \int p_1(\x \mid \x_1) q(\x_1)\d\x_1 =  \cN(\x \mid \x_1,\sigma^2\I) \ast q(\x),
\end{align}
meaning that if $\sigma \ll 1$, then $p_1(\x)\approx q(\x)$.

We now need to identify a corresponding vector field that produces the above probability path; \cite{lipman2023flowmatching} identify this field through the  following result. 

\begin{theorem}[From conditional to marginal vector field]
    \label{thm:lipman_VF}
    If $\v_t(\x \mid \x_1) $ is a conditional velocity field that generates the conditional probability path $p_t(\x \mid \x_1)$, the marginal vector field defined by 
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:marginal_vactor_field}
        \v_t(\x) = \int \v_t(\x \mid \x_1) 
        \frac{p_t(\x \mid \x_1) q(\x_1)}{p_t(\x)}\,\d\x_1, 
    \end{equation}
    generates the marginal probability path in 
    eq.~\eqref{eq:marginal_prob_path}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof follows by showing that the marginal pair 
$ p_t(\x)$ and $\v_t(\x)$ also satisfies the continuity equation.

\begin{align*}
    \partial_t p_t(\x) 
    &= 
    \int \partial_t p_t(\x \mid \x_1)\, q(\x_1)\d\x_1 
    &&\text{(eq.~\eqref{eq:marginal_prob_path} and swap diff/integral)} \\[4pt]
    &= 
    - \int \nabla_\x \cdot 
    \big[ \v_t(\x \mid \x_1) p_t(\x \mid \x_1) \big] 
    q(\x_1)\d\x_1 
    &&\text{(conditional continuity equation)} \\[4pt]
    &= 
    - \nabla_\x \cdot 
    \left[\int 
    \v_t(\x \mid \x_1) p_t(\x \mid \x_1) 
    q(\x_1)\d\x_1 \right] 
    &&\text{(swap diff/integral)} \\[4pt]
    &= 
    - \nabla_\x \cdot 
    \left[
    p_t(\x)\!
    \int \v_t(\x \mid \x_1)
    \frac{p_t(\x \mid \x_1) q(\x_1)}{p_t(\x)}
    \d\x_1
    \right] 
    &&\text{(multiplying and dividing by $p_t(\x)$)} \\[4pt]
    &= 
    - \nabla_\x \cdot 
    \big[p_t(\x)\v_t(\x)\big]
    &&\text{(eq.~\eqref{eq:marginal_vactor_field})} .
\end{align*}

Therefore, $p_t(\x)$ and $\v_t(\x)$ satisfy the continuity equation, which implies that $\v_t(\x)$ generates the marginal probability path. The interchange of derivatives and integrals above is justified under standard regularity assumptions (e.g., smooth vector fields and integrable densities, DCT).
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Conditional flow matching.} Although Theorem \ref{thm:lipman_VF} provides a meaningful construction for the marginal vector field, this is again intractable as it depends on the data distribution $q(\cdot)$. However, we return to the flow-matching loss introduced in eq.~\eqref{eq:FM_loss}  and extend it to learn the conditional vector field. This yields an alternative loss termed conditional flow matching defined by 
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:CFM_loss}    
    \cL_\text{CFM}(\theta) \defeq \mathbb{E}_{t\sim\uni{0,1},\x_1\sim q(\x_1), \x\sim p_t(\x \mid \x_1)}||\v_\theta(\x,t) - \v_t(\x \mid \x_1)||^2.
\end{equation}
Critically, the objective in  eq.~\eqref{eq:CFM_loss} is tractable, as it only requires (sampling) quantities that are available during training. In particular, data points $\x_1 \sim q(\x_1)$ come from the  dataset, samples $\x \sim p_t(\x \mid \x_1)$ are generated from the chosen conditional probability path, and the corresponding conditional
vector field $\v_t(\x \mid \x_1)$ is analytically specified by the path construction. As a result, the expectation in
eq.~\eqref{eq:CFM_loss} can be estimated using Monte Carlo samples without requiring explicit knowledge of the marginal density $p_t(\x)$ or the marginal vector field $\v_t(\x)$.

The result linking this loss to the (marginal) FM loss is presented as follows 

\begin{theorem}
    \label{thm:lipman_CFM}
    If $p_t(\x)>0,\, \forall \x\in\R^D, t\in[0,1]$, then, $\cL_\text{FM}(\theta) = \cL_\text{CFM}(\theta), \forall \theta, $ up to constants that are independent of $\theta$. As a consequence, we have 
    \[
    \nabla_\theta \cL_\text{FM}(\theta) = \nabla_\theta\cL_\text{CFM}(\theta),
    \]
    therefore, the flow-matching model can be trained by stocastich gradient descent on the tractable $\cL_\text{CFM}(\theta)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    See \cite[Appendix]{lipman2023flowmatching}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Choices of the probability path and the associated vector field.} A general construction for the conditional probability path is as follows
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:conditional_Gaussian_PP}
    p_t(\x \mid \x_1) = \cN(\x ; \mu_t(\x_1), \sigma_t^2(\x_1)\I), 
\end{equation}
with $\mu_t$ and $\sigma_t$ time-dependent functions with the following boundary conditions
\begin{align}
    t=0 &:  \mu_0=0,\,\sigma_0= 1\, \Rightarrow p_0(\x) = \cN(\x \mid 0,\I)\\
    t=1 &: \mu_1=\x_1,\,\sigma_1= \sigma_\text{min}\, \Rightarrow p_1(\x) = \cN(\x \mid \x_1,\sigma^2_\text{min}\I),
\end{align}
thus ensuring that the conditional probability path goes from a standard normal on $\R^D$ to a Gaussian concentrated on $\x_1$. Among several alternatives, we choose a flow that generates that probability path in eq.~\eqref{eq:conditional_Gaussian_PP} given by 
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:conditional_Gaussian_flow}
    \phi_t(\x) = \mu_t(\x_1) + \sigma_t(\x_1)\x; \quad \x\sim\cN(0,\I).
\end{equation}



\begin{proposition}
    \label{prop:conditional_vector_field}
    The conditional vector field given by 
    \[
    \v_t (\x \mid \x_1) = \mu_t'(\x_1) + \frac{\sigma_t'(\x_1)}{\sigma_t(\x_1)}\left(\x  - \mu_t(\x_1)\right),
    \]
    generates the flow in eq.~\eqref{eq:conditional_Gaussian_flow}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
    Since the governing ODE is 
\(
\frac{\d}{\dt}\phi_t(\x) = \v_t(\phi_t(\x) \mid \x_1), 
\)
the proof proceeds by calculating the derivative of $\phi_t(\x)$ and expressing it as a function of the the same quantity. From eq.~\eqref{eq:conditional_Gaussian_flow} we know that 
\[
\x = \frac{\phi_t(\x) - \mu_t(\x_1)}{\sigma_t(\x_1)},
\]
combining this with the derivative of eq.~\eqref{eq:conditional_Gaussian_flow}, we have
\begin{align*}
    \frac{\d}{\dt}\phi_t(\x) 
    &= 
    \mu_t'(\x_1) + \sigma_t'(\x_1)\x,\\
    &= 
    \mu_t'(\x_1) + \sigma_t'(\x_1)\frac{\phi_t(\x) - \mu_t(\x_1)}{\sigma_t(\x_1)},
\end{align*}
thus concluding the proof.

    
\end{proof}

The last step in the specification of this flow-matching framework is the analytical expressions for the mean and covariance. Again, among a number of alternatives, we take a natural choice and consider the optimal transport between the source $\cN(0,\I)$ and the (conditional) target $\cN(\x_1,\sigma^2_\text{min}\I)$, thus defining the flow as the geodesic interpolant given by 
\begin{align*}
    \phi_t(\x) 
    &=  (1-t)\phi_0(\x) + t\phi_1(\x) \\
    &=  (1-t)\x + t(\x_1 + \x\sigma_\text{min}) \\ 
    &=  (1-t(1-\sigma_\text{min}))\x + t\x_1.
\end{align*}
This allows for identifying the time-indexed mean and variance as---see eq.~\eqref{eq:conditional_Gaussian_flow} 
\begin{align*}
    \mu_t(\x_1) &= t\x_1,\quad \text{going from $0$ to $\x_1$}\\
    \sigma_t(\x_1) &= 1-t(1-\sigma_\text{min}), \quad \text{going from $1$ to $\sigma_\text{min}$}.
\end{align*}
Evaluating these expressions into the result in Prop.~\ref{prop:conditional_vector_field} gives the conditional vector field as
\begin{align}
\v_t(\x \mid \x_1)
&=
\frac{\x_1 - (1-\sigma_{\min})\,\x}
     {1 - t(1-\sigma_{\min})}.
\end{align}

This gives the conditional flow matching objective
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:CFM_loss_final}
\cL_\text{CFM}(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{t\sim\uni{0,1},\,\x_1\sim q(\x_1),\,\x\sim p_t(\x \mid \x_1)}
\left\|
\v_\theta(\x,t)
-
\frac{\x_1 - (1-\sigma_{\min})\,\x}
     {1 - t(1-\sigma_{\min})}
\right\|^2 .
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}[Conditional vs. marginal optimal transport]
The conditional flow defined above corresponds to the OT geodesic between the Gaussian distributions 
$\cN(0,\I)$ and $\cN(\x_1,\sigma_{\min}^2\I)$ for each fixed terminal point $\x_1$. 
However, this property does not propagate the marginal probability path obtained after averaging over $\x_1 \sim q(\x_1)$. 
Indeed, the marginal path 
\[
p_t(\x) = \int p_t(\x \mid \x_1) q(\x_1)\,\d \x_1
\]
is a mixture of these conditional Gaussian paths, and therefore it is not, in general, the OT geodesic between $p_0(\x)=p(\x)$ and $p_1(\x)\approx q(\x)$.

Ideally, one would prefer the marginal flow itself to follow an OT geodesic, as this yields the most efficient transport, avoiding unnecessary displacement and leading to simpler dynamics for the parametrised vector field which are, arguably, easier to be learnt.
\end{remark}



\paragraph{Rectified flow.} As noted by \cite{tong2023improving}, the conditioning statement given by the terminal value to be a sample of the training dataset used in the formulation above can be relaxed to a more general conditioning statement. This means that, given a RV $\z\sim q(\z)$ and a conditional probability path $\{p_t(\x \mid \z)\}_{t\in[0,1]}$, the corresponding marginal probability path is 
\begin{equation}
    p_t(\x) = \int p_t(\x \mid \z) q(\z) \d\z.
\end{equation}
\begin{remark}
    Theorems \ref{thm:lipman_VF} and  \ref{thm:lipman_CFM} also hold for this general choice of the conditioning statement. 
\end{remark}
\begin{remark}
    Choosing $\z=\x$ recovers the above formulation by \cite{lipman2023flowmatching}
\end{remark}

In addition to the datapoint $\x_1\sim q(\x)$, let us also consider a sample from the source distribution $\x_0\sim p(\x)$. As proposed by \cite{liu2022rectified}, choosing  $\z=(\x_0,\x_1)$ allows one to define the following conditional probability path: 
\begin{equation}
    p_t( \x \mid \z) = \cN(\x \mid t\x_1 + (1-t)\x_0,\sigma^2\I_D). 
\end{equation}
with the associated velocity field
\begin{equation}
    \v_t( \x \mid \z) = \x_1 - \x_0.
\end{equation}
Here, assuming $\sigma<<1$, the conditional probability path evolves from a Gaussian concentrated on a source sample $\x_0$ to a Gaussian concentrated on a target sample $\x_1$. 

\begin{remark}
    Though the velocity field is simple and easy to implement, pairing arbitrary
    samples from $p(\x)$ and $q(\x)$ may lead to inefficient transport with
    trajectories that cross or move mass unnecessarily long distances. This
    arises because the construction implicitly assumes an independent coupling
    $q(\z)=q(\x_0)q(\x_1)$ between source and target samples, ignoring the
    geometry of the two distributions. An alternative is to choose
    $q(\z)=\pi(\x_0,\x_1)$ to be the OT coupling between the
    (empirical) source and target distributions, yielding straight trajectories
    and more efficient transport of probability mass.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Learning the two-moon dataset --- from \cite{lipman2024flowmatchingtutorial}]
Define $\v_\theta(t,\x)$ as a 4-layer neural network with ELU activations. To construct the training samples, first sample a batch of 
\begin{align}
    \text{source (standard Gaussian): }\quad \x_0 &\sim p(\x) = \cN(0,\I),\\
    \text{target (two-moons): }\quad \x_1 &\sim q(\x).
\end{align}
Then, for random timestamps $t \sim \mathrm{Unif}(0,1)$, define the interpolated point
\[
\x_t = (1-t)\x_0 + t\x_1,
\]
which corresponds to the conditional velocity field
\(
\v_t(\x \mid \x_0,\x_1) = \x_1 - \x_0.
\)

The neural network is trained by minimising the mean squared error
\[
\|\v_\theta(t,\x_t) - (\x_1 - \x_0)\|^2,
\]
averaged wrt to the $t,\x_0,\x_1,\x_t$ samples, thus regressing the learned vector field onto the analytical conditional velocity field.

After training, we generate samples by drawing
$\x \sim \cN(0,\I)$ and integrating the learned ODE
\[
\frac{d\x_t}{dt} = \v_\theta(t,\x_t)
\]
from $t=0$ to $t=1$. Figure~\ref{fig:rectified_flow_example} shows the evolution of particles
as they are transported by the learned flow from the Gaussian source
distribution toward the two--moons target distribution.

\end{example}

\begin{exercise}[Change of variables for an affine flow]
Let \(T(\u)=A\u+b\) on \(\mathbb{R}^D\), where \(A\in\mathbb{R}^{D\times D}\) is invertible and \(b\in\mathbb{R}^D\). Assume \(\u\sim p_\u\).

\begin{enumerate}
\item Show that the density of \(\x=T(\u)\) is
\[
p(\x)=p_\u\!\left(A^{-1}(\x-b)\right)\,|\det A|^{-1}.
\]
\item Consider \(p_\u=\mathcal N(0,I)\) and identify the distribution of \(\x\).
\item Write the log-likelihood \(\log p(\x)\) explicitly in terms of \(A\), \(b\), and \(\x\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}[Change of variables for an affine flow]

\textbf{1. Density of $\x = T(\u) = A\u + b$.}

Since $A$ is invertible, the transformation $T$ is bijective. The inverse map is
\[
\u = T^{-1}(\x) = A^{-1}(\x - b),
\]
with Jacobian
\(
\frac{\partial \u}{\partial \x} = A^{-1}.
\)
Using the change-of-variables formula and using $|\det(A^{-1})| = |\det A|^{-1}$ gives 
\[
p(\x) = p_\u\!\left(A^{-1}(\x-b)\right)\,|\det A|^{-1}.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{2. Distribution when $\u \sim \mathcal N(0,I)$.}

If $\u \sim \mathcal N(0,I)$ and $\x = A\u + b$, then an affine transformation of a Gaussian random vector remains Gaussian. The mean and covariance are

\[
\mathbb{E}[\x] = A\mathbb{E}[\u] + b = b,
\qquad
\mathrm{Cov}(\x) = A\,\mathrm{Cov}(\u)\,A^\top = AA^\top.
\]

Therefore
\[
\x \sim \mathcal N(b,\, AA^\top).
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{3. Explicit log-likelihood.}

The density of $\u \sim \mathcal N(0,I)$ is
\[
p_\u(\u) = (2\pi)^{-D/2}\exp\!\left(-\frac12 \u^\top\u\right).
\]

Substituting $\u = A^{-1}(\x-b)$ into the change-of-variables expression and taking the logarithm gives
\[
\log p(\x)
=
-\frac{D}{2}\log(2\pi)
-\frac12 (A^{-1}(\x-b))^\top (A^{-1}(\x-b))
-\log |\det A|.
\]

Equivalently, since $(A^{-1}(\x-b))^\top (A^{-1}(\x-b)) = (\x-b)^\top (AA^\top)^{-1}(\x-b)$,
\[
\log p(\x)
=
-\frac{D}{2}\log(2\pi)
-\frac12 (\x-b)^\top (AA^\top)^{-1}(\x-b)
-\frac12 \log \det(AA^\top).
\]

\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}[A scalar normalising flow via CDF matching]
Let \(u\sim \mathrm{Unif}([0,1])\), and define
\[
x=T(u)=F^{-1}(u),
\]
where \(F\) is the CDF of a strictly positive density \(p\) on \(\mathbb{R}\).

\begin{enumerate}
\item Show that \(T_\# p_u = p\), where \(p_u\) denotes the uniform density on \([0,1]\).
\item Derive \(T'(u)\) in terms of \(p\bigl(T(u)\bigr)\).
\item Recover the density \(p(x)\) from the change-of-variables formula.
\item Apply the construction when \(p\) is the exponential density \(p(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\mathbf{1}_{x\ge 0}\), and compute \(T(u)\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}[A scalar normalising flow via CDF matching]

\textbf{1.} Let $x=T(u)=F^{-1}(u)$ with $u\sim \mathrm{Unif}([0,1])$. Then
\[
\mathbb{P}(x\le t)
= \mathbb{P}(F^{-1}(u)\le t)
= \mathbb{P}(u\le F(t))
= F(t).
\]
Thus the CDF of $x$ is $F$, so $x$ has density $p$. Hence $T_\# p_u = p$.

\medskip

\textbf{2.} Since $F(T(u))=u$, differentiate:
\[
F'(T(u))\,T'(u)=1.
\]
Because $F'(x)=p(x)$,
\[
T'(u)=\frac{1}{p(T(u))}.
\]

\medskip

\textbf{3.} By change of variables,
\[
p(x)=p_u(T^{-1}(x))\left|\frac{d}{dx}T^{-1}(x)\right|.
\]
Here $T^{-1}(x)=F(x)$, $p_u=1$ on $[0,1]$, and
\[
\frac{d}{dx}T^{-1}(x)=F'(x)=p(x),
\]
which recovers the density $p(x)$.

\medskip

\textbf{4.} For $p(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\mathbf{1}_{x\ge0}$,
\[
F(x)=1-e^{-\lambda x}.
\]
Solving $u=F(x)$,
\[
x=T(u)=-\frac{1}{\lambda}\log(1-u), \qquad u\in(0,1).
\]

\end{solution}


\begin{exercise}[A simple coupling layer]
Split \(\u=(u_1,u_2)\in\mathbb{R}^{d_1}\times\mathbb{R}^{d_2}\), and define the map
\[
T(u_1,u_2)=\bigl(x_1,x_2\bigr),\qquad
x_1=u_1,\qquad
x_2=u_2\odot \exp(s(u_1))+t(u_1),
\]
where \(s:\mathbb{R}^{d_1}\to\mathbb{R}^{d_2}\), \(t:\mathbb{R}^{d_1}\to\mathbb{R}^{d_2}\) differentiable, and \(\odot\) denotes componentwise multiplication.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Show that \(T\) is invertible, and compute \(T^{-1}\).
\item Compute the Jacobian matrix of \(T\).
\item Show that
\[
\log |\det \nabla T(u_1,u_2)| = \sum_{j=1}^{d_2} s_j(u_1).
\]
\item Explain why this transformation is attractive for likelihood-based training.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}[A simple coupling layer]

\textbf{1. Inverse.}
Since
\[
x_1=u_1,\qquad x_2=u_2\odot e^{s(u_1)}+t(u_1),
\]
we immediately get
\[
u_1=x_1.
\]
Then
\[
u_2=(x_2-t(x_1))\odot e^{-s(x_1)}.
\]
Hence
\[
T^{-1}(x_1,x_2)
=
\bigl(x_1,\,(x_2-t(x_1))\odot e^{-s(x_1)}\bigr),
\]
so $T$ is invertible.

\medskip

\textbf{2. Jacobian.}
The Jacobian has block form
\[
\nabla T(u_1,u_2)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
I_{d_1} & 0\\[1mm]
* & \mathrm{Diag}(e^{s(u_1)})
\end{pmatrix},
\]
where the lower-left block $*$ is some $d_2\times d_1$ matrix coming from differentiating $x_2$ with respect to $u_1$. This expression has closed form given the derivatives of the functions $s(\cdot)$ and $t(\cdot)$.

\medskip

\textbf{3. Log-determinant.}
Since the Jacobian is block triangular,
\[
\det \nabla T(u_1,u_2)
=
\det(I_{d_1})\det(\mathrm{Diag}(e^{s(u_1)}))
=
\prod_{j=1}^{d_2} e^{s_j(u_1)}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\log |\det \nabla T(u_1,u_2)|
=
\sum_{j=1}^{d_2} s_j(u_1).
\]

\medskip

\textbf{4. Usefulness for likelihood-based  training.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $T$ is easily invertible, so sampling and density evaluation are straightforward, and
    \item the determinant of its  Jacobian is inexpensive to compute, as it reduces to a sum. Therefore, exact log-likelihood training is feasible and efficient.
\end{itemize}

\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}[Composition of flows]
Let \(T=T_K\circ\cdots\circ T_1\) be a composition of diffeomorphisms on \(\mathbb{R}^D\), and define the intermediate variables
\[
\z_0=\u,\qquad \z_k=T_k(\z_{k-1}),\quad k=1,\dots,K,\qquad \z_K=\x.
\]

\begin{enumerate}
\item Using the chain rule, show that
\[
\det \nabla T(\u)=\prod_{k=1}^K \det \nabla T_k(\z_{k-1}).
\]
\item Deduce the identity
\[
\log p(\x)=\log p_\u(\u)-\sum_{k=1}^K \log \left|\det \nabla T_k(\z_{k-1})\right|.
\]
\item Write the corresponding expression using the inverse maps \(\phi_k=T_k^{-1}\).
\item Briefly discuss why one may prefer composing many simple flows instead of learning a single complicated map.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}[Composition of flows]

\textbf{1.} By the chain rule,
\(
\nabla T(\u)
=
\nabla T_K(\z_{K-1})\cdots \nabla T_1(\z_0).
\)
Taking determinants,
\[
\det \nabla T(\u)
=
\prod_{k=1}^K \det \nabla T_k(\z_{k-1}).
\]

\medskip

\textbf{2.} Using part 1 in the change-of-variables formula gives
\[
p(\x)
=
p_\u(\u)\prod_{k=1}^K \left|\det \nabla T_k(\z_{k-1})\right|^{-1}.
\]
Taking logs,
\[
\log p(\x)
=
\log p_\u(\u)
-
\sum_{k=1}^K \log \left|\det \nabla T_k(\z_{k-1})\right|.
\]

\medskip

\textbf{3.} Denoting $\phi_k=T_k^{-1}$, we have 
\(
\u=\phi_1\circ\cdots\circ\phi_K(\x),
\)
and thus
\[
\log p(\x)
=
\log p_\u\!\bigl(\phi_1\circ\cdots\circ\phi_K(\x)\bigr)
+
\sum_{k=1}^K \log \left|\det \nabla \phi_k(\z_k)\right|,
\]
since
\[
\log |\det \nabla \phi_k(\z_k)|
=
-\log |\det \nabla T_k(\z_{k-1})|.
\]

\medskip

\textbf{4.} Composing several (simpler) flows is preferable because each layer can be made invertible with a cheap Jacobian determinant, while the composition is still expressive. This simplifies optimisation, sampling, and exact likelihood evaluation since usually the inverse and derivatives of the simple flows are available in closed form and do not need to be numerically approximated (the maps $\phi_k$ are chosen to have with this property in mind).

\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}[Gaussian paths in continuous-time flows]
Let \(\z_0\sim \mathcal N(m_0,\Sigma_0)\), and consider the linear ODE
\[
\frac{d\z_t}{dt}=A\z_t+b,
\]
where \(A\in\mathbb{R}^{D\times D}\) and \(b\in\mathbb{R}^D\) are constants.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Show that \(\z_t\) can be written explicitly in terms of \(\z_0\), \(A\), and \(b\).
\item Deduce that \(\z_t\) is Gaussian for every \(t\).
\item Derive ODEs for the mean \(m_t=\mathbb E[\z_t]\) and covariance \(\Sigma_t=\mathrm{Cov}(\z_t)\).
\item Solve these ODEs in the case \(A=0\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}
\begin{solution}[Gaussian paths in continuous-time flows]

\textbf{1. Explicit expression for \(\z_t\).}

The solution of
\[
\frac{d\z_t}{dt}=A\z_t+b
\]
can be written as
\[
\z_t = e^{tA}\z_0 + \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}b\,ds,
\]
where
\[
e^{tA}:=\sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(tA)^k}{k!}
\]
is the matrix exponential.

Indeed, differentiating the right-hand side gives (Leibniz rule)
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\left(e^{tA}\z_0 + \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}b\,ds\right)
=
Ae^{tA}\z_0 + b + \int_0^t A e^{(t-s)A}b\,ds
=
A\z_t+b.
\]
Also, at \(t=0\) this expression gives \(\z_t=\z_0\), so it is the required solution.

Furthermore, if \(A\) is invertible, then
\[
\int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}b\,ds
=
A^{-1}(e^{tA}-I)b,
\]
hence
\[
\z_t = e^{tA}\z_0 + A^{-1}(e^{tA}-I)b.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{2. Gaussianity of \(\z_t\).}

From the formula above, \(\z_t\) is an affine function of \(\z_0\):
\[
\z_t = e^{tA}\z_0 + c_t,
\qquad
c_t:=\int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}b\,ds.
\]
Since \(\z_0\sim \mathcal N(m_0,\Sigma_0)\), it follows that \(\z_t\) is Gaussian for every \(t\). More precisely,
\[
\z_t \sim \mathcal N(m_t,\Sigma_t),
\]
with
\[
m_t = e^{tA}m_0 + c_t,
\qquad
\Sigma_t = e^{tA}\Sigma_0 e^{tA^\top}.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{3. ODEs for the mean and covariance.}

Let
\[
m_t=\mathbb E[\z_t].
\]
Taking expectation in the ODE gives
\[
\frac{dm_t}{dt}=Am_t+b.
\]
Here this is justified since \(\z_t\) is an explicit smooth affine function of \(\z_0\), so differentiation may be taken inside the expectation.

Now define the centred process
\[
\widetilde{\z}_t := \z_t-m_t.
\]
Then
\[
\frac{d\widetilde{\z}_t}{dt}
=
A\widetilde{\z}_t.
\]
Since
\[
\Sigma_t=\mathrm{Cov}(\z_t)=\mathbb E\bigl[\widetilde{\z}_t\widetilde{\z}_t^\top\bigr],
\]
we obtain
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\Sigma_t
=
\mathbb E\!\left[\frac{d\widetilde{\z}_t}{dt}\widetilde{\z}_t^\top
+
\widetilde{\z}_t\frac{d\widetilde{\z}_t^\top}{dt}\right]
=
A\Sigma_t+\Sigma_tA^\top.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{4. Case \(A=0\).}

If \(A=0\), then
\[
\frac{d\z_t}{dt}=b,
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
\z_t=\z_0+t\,b.
\]
Hence
\[
\frac{dm_t}{dt}=b,
\qquad
\frac{d\Sigma_t}{dt}=0,
\]
and therefore
\[
m_t=m_0+t\,b,
\qquad
\Sigma_t=\Sigma_0.
\]
Thus
\[
\z_t \sim \mathcal N(m_0+t\,b,\Sigma_0).
\]

\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}[Deriving the continuity equation in one dimension]
Let \(z_t\in\mathbb{R}\) solve
\[
\frac{dz_t}{dt}=g(t,z_t),
\]
and let \(p_t(z)\) denote the density of \(z_t\).

\begin{enumerate}
\item Starting from conservation of mass on an interval \([a,b]\), show that \(p_t\) satisfies
\[
\partial_t p_t(z)+\partial_z\bigl(p_t(z)\,g(t,z)\bigr)=0.
\]
\item Expand the derivative and rewrite the equation as
\[
\partial_t p_t(z)+g(t,z)\,\partial_z p_t(z)+p_t(z)\,\partial_z g(t,z)=0.
\]
\item Interpret the meaning of each term in the equation.
\item Verify the equation for the constant velocity field \(g(t,z)=c\), and identify the resulting density \(p_t\) in terms of \(p_0\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}


\begin{solution}[Deriving the continuity equation in one dimension]

\textbf{1. Conservation of mass on an interval.}
Conservation of mass says that the rate of change of this mass equals the net flux through the boundary:
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\int_a^b p_t(z)\,dz
=
-\Bigl[p_t(z)\,g(t,z)\Bigr]_{z=a}^{z=b}.
\]
Expressing the rhs as an integral: 
\[
\int_a^b \partial_t p_t(z)\,dz
=
-\int_a^b \partial_z\bigl(p_t(z)\,g(t,z)\bigr)\,dz.
\]
Therefore
\[
\int_a^b \Bigl(\partial_t p_t(z)+\partial_z\bigl(p_t(z)\,g(t,z)\bigr)\Bigr)\,dz=0.
\]
Since this holds for every interval \([a,b]\), we obtain
\[
\partial_t p_t(z)+\partial_z\bigl(p_t(z)\,g(t,z)\bigr)=0.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{2. Expanded form.}

Using the product rule,the continuity equation becomes
\[
\partial_t p_t(z)+g(t,z)\,\partial_z p_t(z)+p_t(z)\,\partial_z g(t,z)=0.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{3. Interpretation of the terms.}

The term \(\partial_t p_t(z)\) is the local rate of change of the density at position \(z\).

The term \(g(t,z)\,\partial_z p_t(z)\) corresponds to transport (advection): the density profile is moved along by the velocity field \(g\).

The term \(p_t(z)\,\partial_z g(t,z)\) corresponds to expansion or compression of space. 
If \(\partial_z g>0\), nearby particles move apart and the density decreases; 
if \(\partial_z g<0\), nearby particles move together and the density increases.

Equivalently, the term \(g(t,z)\,\partial_z p_t(z)\) moves the density along the flow,
while the term \(p_t(z)\,\partial_z g(t,z)\) changes the density because the flow locally stretches or compresses space.

\bigskip

\textbf{4. Constant velocity field \(g(t,z)=c\).}

If \(g(t,z)=c\), then \(\partial_z g(t,z)=0\), so the equation becomes
\[
\partial_t p_t(z)+c\,\partial_z p_t(z)=0.
\]
This is the transport equation with constant speed \(c\). Its solution is
\[
p_t(z)=p_0(z-ct).
\]
Indeed,
\[
\partial_t p_t(z)=-c\,p_0'(z-ct),
\qquad
\partial_z p_t(z)=p_0'(z-ct),
\]
so
\[
\partial_t p_t(z)+c\,\partial_z p_t(z)=0.
\]

Thus the density is simply translated at speed \(c\):
\[
p_t(z)=p_0(z-ct).
\]

\end{solution}


\begin{exercise}[Instantaneous change-of-variables formula]
Assume \(\z_t\) follows the ODE
\[
\frac{d\z_t}{dt}=g_\phi(t,\z_t),
\]
and that the density \(p_t\) satisfies the continuity equation
\[
\partial_t p_t(\z)+\nabla_\z\cdot\bigl(p_t(\z)\,g_\phi(t,\z)\bigr)=0.
\]

\begin{enumerate}
\item Use the product rule to expand the divergence term.
\item By differentiating \(p_t(\z_t)\) along trajectories, show that
\[
\frac{d}{dt} p_t(\z_t)
=
-p_t(\z_t)\,\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z_t).
\]
\item Deduce that
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\log p_t(\z_t)
=
-\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z_t).
\]
\item Integrate this identity from \(t=0\) to \(t=1\) to obtain a formula for \(\log p_1(\z_1)\) in terms of \(\log p_0(\z_0)\) and the divergence of the vector field.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}[Instantaneous change-of-variables formula]

\textbf{1. Expanding the divergence term.}

As in the previous exercise, using the product rule for divergence gives 
\[
\partial_t p_t(\z)
+
g_\phi(t,\z)\cdot\nabla_\z p_t(\z)
+
p_t(\z)\,\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z)
=0.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{2. Differentiating \(p_t(\z_t)\) along trajectories.}

By the chain rule,
\[
\frac{d}{dt} p_t(\z_t)
=
\partial_t p_t(\z_t)
+
\nabla_\z p_t(\z_t)\cdot \frac{d\z_t}{dt}.
\]
Since \(\frac{d\z_t}{dt}=g_\phi(t,\z_t)\), this gives
\[
\frac{d}{dt} p_t(\z_t)
=
\partial_t p_t(\z_t)
+
g_\phi(t,\z_t)\cdot\nabla_\z p_t(\z_t).
\]
Using the continuity equation from part 1,
\[
\partial_t p_t + g_\phi\cdot\nabla_\z p_t
=
- p_t\,\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi,
\]
hence
\[
\frac{d}{dt} p_t(\z_t)
=
- p_t(\z_t)\,\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z_t).
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{3. Equation for \(\log p_t(\z_t)\).}

Using
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\log p_t(\z_t)
=
\frac{1}{p_t(\z_t)}\frac{d}{dt} p_t(\z_t),
\]
we obtain
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\log p_t(\z_t)
=
-\nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z_t).
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{4. Integrating from \(t=0\) to \(t=1\).}

Integrating over time, we have 
\[
\log p_1(\z_1)
=
\log p_0(\z_0)
-
\int_0^1 \nabla_\z\cdot g_\phi(t,\z_t)\,dt.
\]

\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}[Conditional flow matching with a Gaussian path]
Fix \(\x_1\in\mathbb{R}^D\), and consider the conditional Gaussian path
\[
p_t(\x\mid \x_1)=\mathcal N\bigl(\x \mid \mu_t(\x_1),\sigma_t(\x_1)^2 I\bigr),
\]
with
\[
\phi_t(\x)=\mu_t(\x_1)+\sigma_t(\x_1)\x,
\qquad \x\sim \mathcal N(0,I).
\]

\begin{enumerate}
\item Show that \(\phi_t(\x)\sim p_t(\cdot\mid \x_1)\).
\item Differentiate \(\phi_t(\x)\) with respect to \(t\), and express the result in terms of \(\x\), \(\mu_t'(\x_1)\), and \(\sigma_t'(\x_1)\).
\item Eliminate \(\x\) using
\[
\x=\frac{\phi_t(\x)-\mu_t(\x_1)}{\sigma_t(\x_1)},
\]
and derive the conditional velocity field
\[
v_t(\x\mid \x_1)
=
\mu_t'(\x_1)
+
\frac{\sigma_t'(\x_1)}{\sigma_t(\x_1)}
\bigl(\x-\mu_t(\x_1)\bigr).
\]
\item Express the result for the particular case 
\[
\mu_t(\x_1)=t\x_1,
\qquad
\sigma_t(\x_1)=1-t(1-\sigma_{\min}),
\]
and simplify \(v_t(\x\mid \x_1)\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}[Conditional flow matching with a Gaussian path]

\textbf{1. Showing that \(\phi_t(\x)\sim p_t(\cdot\mid \x_1)\).}

Since $\phi_t(\cdot)$ is an affine transformation of the Gaussian random variable  $\x$, it is again Gaussian. Calculating its mean and variance are direct, therefore:  
\[
\phi_t(\x)\sim \mathcal N\bigl(\mu_t(\x_1),\sigma_t(\x_1)^2 I\bigr).
\]
Hence
\[
\phi_t(\x)\sim p_t(\cdot\mid \x_1).
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{2. Differentiating \(\phi_t(\x)\) with respect to \(t\).}

Differentiating with respect to \(t\), we obtain
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\phi_t(\x)
=
\mu_t'(\x_1)+\sigma_t'(\x_1)\x.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{3. Deriving the conditional velocity field.}

Using
\[
\x=\frac{\phi_t(\x)-\mu_t(\x_1)}{\sigma_t(\x_1)},
\]
we substitute into the previous expression:
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\phi_t(\x)
=
\mu_t'(\x_1)
+
\sigma_t'(\x_1)\frac{\phi_t(\x)-\mu_t(\x_1)}{\sigma_t(\x_1)}.
\]
Therefore the velocity field at a point \(\tilde\x\) is
\[
v_t(\x\mid \x_1)
=
\mu_t'(\x_1)
+
\frac{\sigma_t'(\x_1)}{\sigma_t(\x_1)}
\bigl(\tilde\x-\mu_t(\x_1)\bigr).
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{4. Special case \(\mu_t(\x_1)=t\x_1\) and \(\sigma_t(\x_1)=1-t(1-\sigma_{\min})\).}

Here, we have
\[
\mu_t'(\x_1)=\x_1,
\qquad
\sigma_t'(\x_1)=-(1-\sigma_{\min}).
\]
Substituting into the formula above gives
\[
v_t(\tilde\x\mid \x_1)
=
\x_1
-
\frac{1-\sigma_{\min}}{1-t(1-\sigma_{\min})}
(\tilde\x-t\x_1).
\]

\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}[Rectified flow and transport efficiency]
Consider the conditional path
\[
p_t(\x\mid \x_0,\x_1)=\mathcal N\bigl(\x \mid (1-t)\x_0+t\x_1,\sigma^2 I\bigr),
\]
where \(\x_0\sim p(\x)\) and \(\x_1\sim q(\x)\), and \(\sigma>0\) is small.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Show that the associated conditional velocity field is
\[
v_t(\x\mid \x_0,\x_1)=\x_1-\x_0.
\]
\item Explain why this vector field does not depend on either \(t\) or \(\x\).
\item In the limit \(\sigma\to 0\), describe the resulting trajectories.
\item Consider the one-dimensional case where
\[
p=\tfrac12\delta_{-1}+\tfrac12\delta_{1},
\qquad
q=\tfrac12\delta_{-2}+\tfrac12\delta_{2}.
\]
Compare the trajectories obtained from:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the independent coupling \(q(\x_0,\x_1)=p(\x_0)\,q(\x_1)\),
\item the monotone/OT coupling pairing \(-1\mapsto -2\) and \(1\mapsto 2\).
\end{enumerate}
Which one gives more efficient transport, and why?
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}[Rectified flow and transport efficiency]

\textbf{1. Conditional velocity field.}

Write the path as (following the previous exercise)
\[
\phi_t(\varepsilon)=(1-t)\x_0+t\x_1+\sigma \varepsilon,
\qquad
\varepsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I).
\]
Then
\[
\phi_t(\varepsilon)\sim \mathcal N\bigl((1-t)\x_0+t\x_1,\sigma^2I\bigr),
\]
and differentiating with respect to \(t\) gives
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\phi_t(\varepsilon)=\x_1-\x_0.
\]
Hence the associated conditional velocity field is
\[
v_t(\x\mid \x_0,\x_1)=\x_1-\x_0.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{2. Why it does not depend on \(t\) or \(\x\).}

The mean path
\[
(1-t)\x_0+t\x_1
\]
is a straight line from \(\x_0\) to \(\x_1\). Moving along a straight line at constant speed means the velocity is constant, equal to \(\x_1-\x_0\). The noise term \(\sigma\varepsilon\) does not depend on \(t\), so it does not affect the velocity. 

Intuitively, all particles associated with the pair \((\x_0,\x_1)\) move in parallel straight lines from \(\x_0\) to \(\x_1\), so the velocity field is the same everywhere and at all times for that pair.

\bigskip

\textbf{3. Limit \(\sigma\to 0\).}

As \(\sigma\to 0\), the Gaussian path concentrates on the deterministic line segment (the mean path mentioned above)
\[
\x_t=(1-t)\x_0+t\x_1.
\]
Thus trajectories become straight lines joining \(\x_0\) to \(\x_1\), travelling at constant speed.

\bigskip

\textbf{4. One-dimensional example.}

Here
\[
p=\tfrac12\delta_{-1}+\tfrac12\delta_{1},
\qquad
q=\tfrac12\delta_{-2}+\tfrac12\delta_{2}.
\]

\emph{(a) Independent coupling.}
All four pairings occur with probability \(1/4\):
\[
-1\mapsto -2,\qquad -1\mapsto 2,\qquad 1\mapsto -2,\qquad 1\mapsto 2.
\]
So some particles move a short distance \(1\), while others move a longer distance \(3\), including crossings through the origin.

\emph{(b) Monotone/OT coupling.}
The coupling pairs
\[
-1\mapsto -2,
\qquad
1\mapsto 2.
\]
Thus both trajectories are straight and monotone, each moving distance \(1\).

Therefore the monotone/OT coupling gives more efficient transport, since it avoids unnecessary crossings and minimises the total displacement.

\end{solution}
